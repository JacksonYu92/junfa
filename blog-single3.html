How is Glass Tempered?

How is Glass Tempered?

  • China Glass Network
  • Feb 15, 2020

Tempered glass is essentially a type of prestressed glass. To enhance its strength, chemical or physical methods are commonly employed to create compressive stresses on the glass surface. When subjected to external forces, the compressive stress neutralizes surface tension, thereby increasing its load-bearing capacity and improving its tensile strength.

Tempered glass has two primary advantages. Firstly, it boasts significantly enhanced strength compared to ordinary glass. Its bending strength is 3 to 5 times that of regular glass, and its impact resistance is 5 to 10 times higher. This not only increases its strength but also enhances safety. The second main advantage is improved safety. The increased load-bearing capacity reduces fragility. Even in the event of breakage, tempered glass shatters into small, blunt fragments, minimizing harm to humans. Tempered glass has 2 to 3 times greater resistance to rapid temperature changes compared to regular glass, typically withstanding temperature differences of over 150°C. This property significantly reduces the risk of thermal stress fractures.

Tempered glass is processed from standard flat glass or float glass. For flat glass, selected or first-rate products are required, while float glass necessitates the use of excellent or first-grade products.

There are two main production methods for tempered glass. One involves subjecting standard flat glass or float glass to specific processing conditions, followed by quenching or air-cooling. The other method involves altering the composition of the glass surface through ion exchange on standard flat glass or float glass, resulting in the creation of a layer of compressive stress.

Tempered glass boasts features such as high impact resistance (4 to 5 times that of standard flat glass), high bending strength (5 times that of standard flat glass), good thermal stability, and attributes like smoothness, transparency, and the ability to be cut. When it shatters under intense impact, the fragments are dispersed into fine particles with no sharp edges, earning it the name "safety glass."

Tempered glass can be categorized into flat tempered glass and curved tempered glass based on shape. The thicknesses of flat tempered glass include 4, 5, 6, 8, 10, 12, 15, and 19 mm. Curved tempered glass comes in three thicknesses: 5, 6, and 8 mm.

According to appearance quality, tempered glass can be classified into two categories: superior and qualified.

Tempered glass can be classified based on fragment status into classes I, II, and III.
